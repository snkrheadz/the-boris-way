# Craft: Career (resumes, portfolios, interviews, job search, promotions)

What excellent career work IS, checkable. Negotiation mechanics live in sales.md section 5;
this file applies them to the job context.

## 1. Resumes and CVs

- Achievement bullets, never duty lists: did X, measured by Y, via Z ("cut render time 60%
  by moving encoding to a worker pool"), not "responsible for rendering pipeline".
- Every bullet carries a number or a named outcome; where metrics are genuinely absent, scope
  works (team size, budget, users, timeline). Every number survives "how did you measure
  that?" in an interview; round multipliers (10x, 99%) read as invented.
- Tailor to the job description's actual nouns: screeners and ATS systems match words, but
  the nouns live inside achievement bullets showing use, never in a keyword-stuffed skills
  block. One master resume, one tailored variant per application.
- The top third of page one decides: a screener gives it seconds, so the strongest relevant
  material leads. Recency detailed, history compressed; one page per decade as the default.
- Resume, LinkedIn, and application forms agree on titles and dates; discrepancies surface
  in background checks.
- No photo, no "objective", no "references available on request", no skills-bar graphics.
- Honest absolutely: no inflated titles, stretched dates, or claimed solo credit for team
  work. Fabrication is discoverable and fatal; framing is fair game, fiction is not.

## 2. Portfolio and proof

- 3-5 case studies beat 20 thumbnails. Each: the problem, the constraints, YOUR specific
  role, the decisions, the outcome with numbers.
- Show the process (options considered, the messy middle), not just the polished final;
  process is what separates authors from downloaders.
- Public and linked everywhere; a claim with a link outweighs a paragraph of adjectives.
- Be ready to defend every piece in depth; never include work you cannot explain.

## 3. Cover letters and outreach

- Specific to THIS company: name the product detail, decision, or writing that genuinely
  hooked you, something deeper than the homepage headline. The test: the sentence would be
  false sent to their nearest competitor (generic letters read in one second as generic).
- One proof story matched to their stated need, then out; under 200 words wins.
- Cold outreach follows sales.md section 1 discovery rules: their problem first, your
  relevance second, a small specific ask ("15 minutes about how you handle X"), never "any
  opportunities?".
- Warm referrals outperform cold applications by an order of magnitude: ask specifically
  ("could you introduce me to <name> about the <role>?"), and make it easy (send them a
  3-line blurb they can forward).

## 4. Interviews

- Prepare 6-8 STAR stories (situation, task, action, result) covering: a conflict, a failure,
  leading without authority, ambiguity, a hard tradeoff, your best win. Rehearse ALOUD with
  timing; two minutes per answer, result stated with numbers.
- "I" not "we" for your actions; interviewers grade your contribution, not your team's.
- Failure questions get a real failure, the real lesson, and the changed behavior since;
  a costume weakness ("I just work too hard") signals dishonesty.
- Technical rounds: clarify the question before solving, think aloud, state tradeoffs, test
  your own answer before declaring done (code.md gate thinking applies).
- Your questions for them are prepared and real (about the work, the team's bottlenecks, how
  success is measured); "no questions" reads as no interest.
- Close by asking the next step and its date; same-day thank-you note referencing one
  specific moment from the conversation.

## 5. Offers, negotiation, promotions

- Never accept on the call: gratitude, enthusiasm, and 24-72 hours to review, always.
- Negotiate with market data and competing signal; total compensation (equity, bonus, remote,
  title, start date), not base alone; trades not demands (sales.md section 5); final offer in
  writing before resigning anywhere.
- Keep a brag document continuously: dated wins with impact, updated monthly; memory drops
  your best material.
- Make wins visible as they land (a demo, a written update, the metric in the team channel),
  not only at review time; the brag document is the archive, not the campaign.
- A promotion case = evidence of already operating at the next level, mapped to the written
  criteria for it. Arm your manager: they present your case in rooms you are not in, so hand
  them the document that makes it easy.

## 6. Job search operations

- Run it like a pipeline (sales.md section 6): stages, next actions with dates, weekly
  review; applications without follow-ups are wishes.
- Target list first: pick 20-40 companies that fit, work a referral path into each, and let
  postings supplement; purely reactive applying competes only for the jobs everyone sees.
- Volume with tailoring beats either alone: a tailored resume plus a referral attempt per
  application.
- Brief your references before they can be called: the role, the claims to reinforce, a
  heads-up per call; an unprepared reference is a coin flip.
- Rejections are throughput; review only for patterns (same stage repeatedly = fix that
  stage), never for self-flagellation.

## 7. Ban list

- Duty-list bullets; "results-oriented team player with a passion for excellence" fluff.
- Resume verb slop ("spearheaded", "leveraged", "utilized", "passionate", "proven track
  record"): plain verb plus what changed.
- "I am writing to express my interest" and "my skills align perfectly" openers: open with
  the matched proof story instead.
- Any fabrication: titles, dates, credentials, metrics.
- One generic resume sprayed everywhere; objectives paragraphs; skills bars.
- Trash-talking a previous employer in any interview, ever (it transfers to you).
- Accepting the first number on the spot; negotiating with entitlement instead of evidence.
- Portfolio filler you cannot defend; group work presented as solo.

## 8. Verification checklist (every pass)

1. Every resume bullet: action + measurable outcome? Read each aloud and ask "so what?".
2. Noun-overlap check between resume and the target job description performed?
3. Top third of page one carries the strongest material? Titles and dates match LinkedIn?
4. STAR stories: rehearsed aloud, timed, results quantified?
5. Each application: tailored artifact + referral attempt + dated follow-up?
6. Brag document updated within the last month? References briefed?
7. Offer handling: nothing accepted same-day, everything final in writing?
