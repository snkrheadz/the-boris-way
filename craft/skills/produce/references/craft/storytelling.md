# Craft: Storytelling (fiction, narrative, scripts, brand stories, case studies)

What excellent narrative IS, checkable. writing.md is clarity and persuasion; stories run on
different physics.

## 1. Character

- Want vs need: the external goal the character chases, and the internal truth they must face.
  The gap between them is the story's engine.
- The flaw causes their mistakes; obstacles that come only from outside are a weather report,
  not a plot.
- Distinct voice per character: cover the name on any dialogue line and a reader should still
  know who speaks (vocabulary, rhythm, what they notice, what they avoid saying). A pasted-on
  verbal tic or accent is a costume, not a voice.
- Characters act from motive, never from plot convenience. If someone must act stupidly for
  the plot to work, the plot is broken.

## 2. Structure

- Hook: a disturbance to normal life within the first page or minute; start as close to the
  trouble as possible.
- Escalation: each obstacle costs more and cuts closer to the flaw than the last; a flat
  middle means the stakes stopped compounding. Check: list the obstacles with their costs in
  order; if two adjacent ones could swap without loss, the middle is flat.
- Midpoint turns the goal (what they wanted becomes impossible, insufficient, or wrong).
- Climax: want and need collide, and the choice reveals who they have become. Dramatize it on
  the page: a climax summarized, skipped, or talked away deflates the whole story.
- The ending pays the opening's promise: setups get payoffs, and the final image answers the
  first one. Resolution costs something visible; an ending where nothing was permanently lost
  or changed deflates the stakes retroactively.
- Pacing: word count tracks importance. Summarize transit, dramatize decisions; if a
  transition scene outweighs the turn it leads to, rebalance.
- Scene mechanics: every scene has a goal, a conflict, and a change (a "value shift": the
  character exits better or worse than they entered). Name the shift as a one-line polarity
  flip (enters hopeful, exits betrayed); "they talk and learn things" is not a change. A
  scene with no change is description.

## 3. Show, don't tell (where it matters)

- Emotion through action and specifics ("she reread the text four times, then put the phone
  in the drawer") not labels ("she was anxious").
- One or two chosen sensory details per beat do more than ten sprayed ones; the specific detail
  ("the fan's tick") outworks the general one ("the noisy room").
- Telling is allowed for transitions and pace; showing is spent where the emotion peaks.

## 4. Dialogue

- Subtext: people rarely say exactly what they mean, especially under pressure; the gap
  between said and meant is where dialogue lives.
- No exposition dumps ("as you know, brother, our father..."); information arrives because a
  character needs it now, or through friction.
- Every line does two jobs (reveals character AND moves the scene); lines doing neither die.
- Tags invisible: "said" and action beats; adverb-propped tags ("she exclaimed angrily")
  signal the line failed.
- The read-aloud test, always.

## 5. POV and information

- One point of view per scene; the narration knows only what the POV can know.
- Psychic distance consistent (do not head-hop or lurch from cinematic to intimate mid-beat).
- Tension is managed information: what the reader knows vs what characters know (dramatic
  irony), what is withheld and when it is revealed. Withholding without promising is
  confusion; promising and paying late is suspense.
- Stakes explicit: the reader must know what failure costs and why it must happen now.

## 6. Brand stories and case studies (business narrative)

- The customer is the hero; the product is the tool, at most the mentor. A story where the
  brand is the hero is an ad wearing a costume.
- Real specifics or nothing: named moments, honest numbers, actual quotes. Invented-sounding
  smoothness kills belief.
- Include the trouble: what almost failed, the doubt, the workaround. Conflict is what makes
  it a story instead of a testimonial. The trouble must cost something real; trouble that
  flatters the product ("our only problem was choosing a plan") is an ad tell, not conflict.
- The turn shows the first concrete use (who did what, what changed that week), never a
  discovery montage ("that's when they found X").
- Shape: normal, disturbance, struggle, turn (where the product earns its place), new normal
  with the measurable difference.

## 7. Revision

- Draft fast; revise in passes: structure first (cut a scene, does the story break? no =
  cut it), then character consistency, then line-level last. Polishing a scene that should
  die is the classic waste.
- Kill darlings that serve the author, not the story.
- Read the whole thing aloud once; the ear catches what the eye forgives.

## 8. Ban list

- Waking-up openings, weather openings, mirror descriptions of the protagonist.
- Dream sequences that erase stakes; "it was all a dream" endings; deus ex machina rescues.
- Flawless heroes and motiveless villains.
- Purple prose (three adjectives doing one adjective's job); thesaurus words for their own sake.
- Moralizing final paragraphs that explain the theme ("and so she learned...").
- "Little did they know" narrator winks; coincidence that SOLVES problems (coincidence may
  cause them).
- Emotion labeled then shown ("She was furious. She slammed the door."): cut the label.
- Looping physical beats (nodded, smiled, sighed, took a deep breath) and "let out a breath
  they didn't know they were holding": one physical beat per exchange, and it characterizes.
- Fantasy-default names (Elara, Kael, Lyra, Seraphina) and stock casts (Sarah Chen, Marcus):
  names carry culture, class, and era; cast from the story's actual world.
- Stock atmosphere ("the air was thick with tension", "a chill ran down her spine") -> a
  detail only this room could produce; at most one simile per page, from the POV's world.
- Theme spoken aloud at the climax (the epiphany speech); the final choice carries the theme.
- Every scene exiting on the same one-line zinger cadence; vary the exits.

## 9. Verification checklist (every pass)

1. Hook inside the first unit? Name the disturbance.
2. For each scene: goal, conflict, and the value shift named? Cut candidates identified?
3. Want and need stated in one line each; does the climax force them to collide?
4. Cover-the-name dialogue test passed on the main cast?
5. Stakes and the ticking clock explicit somewhere early?
6. Setups listed and each paid off? Ending answers the opening and pays a visible cost?
7. Climax dramatized on the page, not summarized or talked away?
8. Ban-list scan: labeled emotion, looping beats, zinger exits, default names?
9. Case studies: real numbers, real quotes, the trouble included, customer as hero?
