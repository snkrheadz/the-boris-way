# Craft: Sales (selling, demos, proposals, negotiation, partnerships, customer conversations)

What excellent selling and customer interaction IS, checkable. Marketing (craft/marketing.md)
is one-to-many; this file is one-to-one.

## 1. Qualification and discovery

- Sell the problem solved, never the product. Discovery comes before any pitch.
- Qualify explicitly: is there a real need, a trigger that makes it urgent NOW, someone with
  authority and budget, and a timeline? Disqualify fast and kindly (by the second
  conversation, not the fifth); a fast no beats a slow no, and a bad-fit customer costs more
  than no customer.
- Pre-call floor: their product, their role, one recent specific event, ten minutes minimum.
  A question their homepage answers burns goodwill you cannot buy back.
- Discovery craft: open questions about their current process, what it costs them (hours,
  money, risk, missed deals), what they already tried and why it failed. Listen 70, talk 30.
- Write their exact words down. Their vocabulary becomes the demo script, the proposal, and
  eventually the marketing copy.

## 2. Demo craft

- Demo THEIR scenario, not your feature tour: their data shapes, their workflow steps, their
  success metric on screen, each traceable to a discovery answer. Swapping their logo into
  the standard tour is not their scenario (realistic names and volumes, per the ban lists).
- Structure: their current pain restated in one line, then the after-state within the first
  five minutes. Land the wow, then stop; do not tour the settings.
- Narrate by outcome, not by click ("this is where you stop paying for the report nobody
  reads", not "here is the export button").
- Every demo ends with a concrete next step and a date, agreed in the call.

## 3. Objections

- The loop: restate the objection in their words until they confirm you have it, isolate it
  ("if we solved that, is anything else in the way?"), answer with evidence (a number, a
  customer story, a live proof), confirm it is resolved.
- A price objection usually means value was not established: return to the cost of their
  problem before defending the number.
- "We need to think about it" gets a gentle isolation ("of course; usually that means either
  price, timing, or a missing capability, which is it closest to?").
- Concede what is true. Credibility on the weak points buys belief on the strong ones.

## 4. Proposals

- One page beats ten: their situation (in their words), the cost of the problem (their
  numbers), the proposed outcome, the plan with dates, the price, and one next step.
- 2-3 options with an honest anchor (product.md pricing rules apply); name the recommended one
  and say why.
- Send same-day while the conversation is warm; every proposal has an expiry or a review date,
  honestly set (no fake countdowns).

## 5. Negotiation (deals, vendors, partnerships, contractors)

- Know your walk-away point and your alternatives BEFORE the conversation; a negotiation
  without a walk-away is a request.
- Anchor first when you are informed; let them anchor when you are not.
- Never concede without a trade: price moves buy something (longer commitment, a case study,
  a referral, scope reduction). Trade what is cheap for you and valuable to them.
- Silence after a number is a tool; the first one to fill it usually pays.
- Same-day written summary of anything agreed verbally; memories diverge within a week.

## 6. Pipeline honesty and follow-up

- Stage deals by BUYER action (agreed to trial, sent security questionnaire), never by seller
  optimism ("feels close").
- Multi-thread every real deal: champion, economic buyer, and likely blocker known by name.
  A single-contact deal is one vacation away from dead; arm the champion with the one-page
  internal pitch they need in rooms you are not in.
- Every open conversation has a dated next step or it is closed-lost; zombie deals corrupt
  the forecast and the focus. Log a loss reason on every closed-lost (price, timing, missing
  capability, no decision); review the tally monthly, it is free positioning research.
- Section 5's same-day written summary applies to every call, not only negotiations: what
  you heard, what was agreed, the next step with its date.
- Follow-ups add value every time (a relevant insight, a shipped fix, an example), never
  "just checking in". Test: the message would make no sense sent to a different prospect.

## 7. Partnerships

- Value flow stated in both directions, in numbers, before any integration work ("we send you
  N leads a month, you give our users X").
- Pilot small and time-boxed (30-90 days) before integrating deep; define numeric success
  metrics and exit terms at the start, while everyone is friendly.

## 8. Support and customer conversations (the retention side of sales)

- Respond fast even when incomplete: "received, reproducing it now, update by 3pm" beats a
  perfect answer tomorrow.
- Reproduce before diagnosing; give a workaround first, then the real fix; close the loop
  personally when it ships ("this is live now, thanks for catching it").
- Angry customer protocol: acknowledge the impact first, in plain words, before any
  explanation. Never argue with the feeling; fix the fact.
- Support patterns are product input: recurring questions are missing UX or missing docs;
  route them to product.md's evidence pile.

## 9. Ban list

- Feature-dump pitches; demos of settings pages; slideware before discovery.
- Fake deadlines, fake discounts, pressure tactics; they close one deal and salt the market.
- Trash-talking competitors (position against them factually; contempt reads as fear).
- Discounting at the first pushback; conceding without a trade.
- "Just checking in", "bumping this", "per my last email", "I hope this email finds you
  well", "quick question" subjects: open with their problem or do not send.
- Fake personalization: a scraped fact welded to a generic pitch ("Loved your post on X.
  Anyway, we help companies like yours..."). Real personalization changes the offer or the
  proof, not the greeting.
- Asking a cold prospect for 15-30 minutes as the first touch: give something useful first,
  then make the smallest ask that advances.
- ROI math built on your assumed numbers presented as theirs: build on the buyer's own
  numbers or label it illustrative.
- Over-promising the roadmap to close a deal (it becomes a churn event with interest).
- Hiding pricing when the buyer is ready to see it.

## 10. Verification checklist (every pass)

1. Qualification recorded (need, trigger, authority, timeline) for every active deal?
2. Demo built on their scenario and their vocabulary, each beat traceable to a discovery
   answer? Ends with a dated next step?
3. Proposal: one page, their words and numbers, options anchored, sent same-day?
4. Negotiation prep: walk-away and trades listed before the call?
5. Pipeline: every deal staged by buyer action, dated next step, more than one named
   contact, loss reasons logged?
6. Support threads: first response time, workaround given, loop closed on fix?
