# Craft: Management (leading people, delegation, feedback, 1:1s, performance, hiring, meetings)

What excellent people leadership IS, checkable.

## 1. Delegation

- Delegate outcomes with constraints, never task lists: what done looks like, the hard limits
  (budget, deadline, non-goals), and the check-in points. How they get there is theirs.
- Hand over context with the task: the why, the history, the stakeholder map, and the access.
  Withholding context sets the person up to re-derive what you already knew.
- State the authority level explicitly per delegation: do and report, propose then do, or ask
  first. Delegating work while keeping every decision is assigning errands, not delegating.
- Match the check-in cadence to risk and track record (new person or high stakes: checkpoints
  at 25/50/75% of the timeline, dates written at handoff; proven person on familiar ground:
  midpoint only), then honor it; surprise inspections teach people to hide problems.

## 2. Feedback

- Specific, behavioral, timely: situation, the observed behavior, its impact ("in the client
  call yesterday, when the pricing question came, the answer wandered; they left unsure").
  Never traits ("you are careless").
- Correct in private, praise in public (and make praise as specific as correction).
- Within 48 hours or the moment is gone; stored-up feedback becomes an ambush. Content-free
  praise ("good job") does not stop the 48h clock; only situation-behavior-impact counts.
- No sandwiches: burying the message between compliments teaches people to fear compliments.
  Say the thing, kindly and plainly, then help.
- Feedback is a dialogue, not a form: end by asking for their read, and change your view when
  their facts are better. The test: could anything they said have changed your mind?

## 3. 1:1s

- Their meeting first: their agenda leads, your topics second. It is not a status meeting
  (status goes in writing). If you spoke more than half the time, it was your meeting.
- Recurring and protected: cancelling twice in a row tells them where they rank.
- Keep running notes per person (topics, commitments, growth threads); revisit career
  direction monthly, not never.
- The exit-interview test: nothing a leaver says should surprise you; if it does, the 1:1s
  were status meetings in disguise.

## 4. Performance

- Expectations written BEFORE judging against them; you cannot grade against an unstated bar.
- The no-surprises rule: nothing appears in a review that was not said earlier, dated, in a
  normal conversation. A surprise review is a management failure, not an employee one.
- Document patterns with dated examples as they happen; memory flattens into vibes.
- Diagnose skill versus will before the plan: skill gaps get training, pairing, and scoped
  work; will gaps get expectations and consequences. One plan for both fails both.
- Act on sustained underperformance in weeks, not quarters (sustained = the same gap named in
  two dated conversations): a clear conversation (the gap, the bar, the support, the
  timeline). Kind and clear beats nice and vague; vague kindness steals someone's chance to
  fix it.

## 5. Hiring

- Job description = the outcomes of a successful first year, not a laundry list of
  requirements (long requirement lists filter for confidence, not ability).
- Structured interviews: same core questions per candidate, a scorecard with defined signals,
  scores written BEFORE comparing notes (group discussion first = the loudest voice hires).
- A small, paid, realistic work sample (2-4 hours, cut from the actual job) beats any
  interview where feasible.
- References with behavioral questions ("tell me about a deadline they missed; what
  happened?"), not verification theater.
- Hold the bar as the req ages: an empty seat costs less than a mis-hire; never lower the
  scorecard because the search is long.
- Close like sales.md: honest selling, fast responses, a real answer to "why here".

## 6. Meetings

- Every meeting has an owner, a stated purpose (decide, create, or inform), and an artifact
  out (a decision with owner and date, a doc, a list). No artifact, no meeting.
- Decide meetings name the decider in the invite; a decision meeting without a named decider
  is an inform meeting in costume.
- Agenda in the invite; if there is a doc, silent reading beats a presented deck.
- Decisions recorded same-day where everyone can see them; an unrecorded decision will be
  re-litigated.
- Default short (25/50); recurring meetings audited quarterly with cancel as the default:
  each survives only if its owner names the artifact it produced last quarter.
- If it could be a message or a doc, it is one.

## 7. Process and escalation

- Add the smallest process that prevents an OBSERVED failure; process built for imagined
  failures is bureaucracy. Remove process that stopped earning its cost.
- Write things down (decision logs, runbooks, onboarding docs) so nothing depends on one
  person's memory; the bus factor is a management metric.
- Define the escalation path and celebrate its use; escalations are information, not failure.
  Answer them within one business day; months of zero escalations means problems are hiding,
  not absent.

## 8. Ban list

- Feedback sandwiches; surprise reviews; public corrections.
- "Culture fit" as an undefined criterion (it decays into "like me"); gut-only hiring.
- Status meetings a message could replace; meetings without artifacts.
- Delegation with secret veto (letting them decide, then overriding quietly).
- Taking work back at the first stumble (it teaches people to escalate everything); coach
  through the miss, reclaim only after an agreed checkpoint fails twice.
- Activity as a performance signal (hours online, message volume, visible busyness); judge
  shipped outcomes against the written bar.
- Praise inflation (everything is "amazing"); reserve strong words for above-bar work or
  praise buys nothing.
- Vague direction ("do better", "be more strategic") without behavioral specifics.
- Keeping a known mis-hire or sustained underperformer in role for months out of avoidance.

## 9. Verification checklist (every pass)

1. Each delegation has outcome, constraints, authority level, context, and check-ins written?
2. Feedback given within 48h, in situation-behavior-impact form, with their read heard?
3. Review content: every point traceable to a prior dated conversation?
4. Underperformance: skill-or-will diagnosed, gap named in dated conversations, timeline set?
5. Interviews: scorecards defined before, filled independently before comparing?
6. This week's meetings: owner, purpose, decider where deciding, artifact for each?
7. Decisions and runbooks written where the team can find them?
