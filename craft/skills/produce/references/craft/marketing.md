# Craft: Marketing (funnels, SEO, AEO, ads, email programs, CRO, launches, social)

What excellent growth mechanics ARE, checkable. The words themselves are craft/writing.md;
this file is the machinery around them.

## 1. Funnel first

- Every asset names the stage it serves (aware, consider, convert, activate, retain, refer)
  and the ONE metric it moves. An asset serving "everything" serves nothing.
- One primary CTA per asset; secondary actions visually and rhetorically subordinate.
- Message match down the chain: the ad's promise is the landing headline is the CTA's verb;
  same offer, audience, and claim, NOT one sentence pasted three times, each step adding new
  information (proof, mechanism, price). A visitor never wonders if they landed right.
- Fix the biggest leak first: measure conversion at every step, rank the drop-offs, work the
  worst one. Adding traffic above a leaking step multiplies the leak.

## 2. SEO

- One page, one search intent. Two intents means two pages; the same intent on two pages
  means they cannibalize.
- Search the target query before writing and match the format the SERP already rewards: if
  the top results are tools, listicles, or comparison tables, a think piece will not rank.
- Title tag up to 60 characters carrying the claim; meta description written as ad copy (it
  is one); H2s phrased as the questions people actually search, pulled from real query data
  (Search Console, autocomplete, People Also Ask, sales-call questions), never invented.
- Internal links with descriptive anchors ("pricing comparison", not "click here"); orphan
  pages get found by nobody.
- Technical floor: canonical tags, sitemap, honest schema markup (FAQ, product, article),
  clean status codes, mobile-first, Core Web Vitals green (ops.md budgets apply).
- Programmatic SEO only when EACH generated page has unique, real value a human would
  bookmark. Spot-check three random generated pages against each other: if only the
  substituted noun differs, they are thin, and thin pages at scale are a penalty.

## 3. AEO (being cited by AI assistants)

- Answer the query in the first 80 words of the page, directly, then elaborate.
- Dated, quotable, specific fact lines ("As of July 2026, X costs $49/mo for 3 seats") are
  what engines lift; vague prose is not liftable.
- FAQ blocks with real questions phrased as users ask them (sourced from support tickets and
  sales calls); consistent entity naming (one product name, spelled one way, everywhere).
- Engines cite third-party pages more than vendor pages: get named in the comparison posts
  and review threads they lift from; verify by asking the assistants the money question.

## 4. Paid ads

- One audience x one pain x one proof per ad. Stacking messages dilutes all of them.
- Creative volume wins: test 5+ genuinely different angles (different pain, different proof,
  different format, not recolors of one idea); kill a variant once it spends 1-2x target
  CPA with zero conversions; feed winners.
- Track to revenue, not clicks: consistent UTMs end to end, conversion events on the money
  action, and a weekly look at cost per real outcome (trial, sub), not CPM vanity.
- Landing page continuity is part of the ad (Section 1); sending paid traffic to a generic
  homepage burns budget.

## 5. Email programs

- This section is one-to-many lifecycle email; one-to-one cold outreach follows sales.md.
- Deliverability floor before anything clever: SPF, DKIM, DMARC set; new domains warmed over
  2-4 weeks before real volume; list hygiene (bounces and dead addresses pruned); visible
  one-click unsubscribe. Never a bought list; it poisons the domain.
- One email, one idea, one ask. A sequence advances one belief per message toward the action.
- Segment by lifecycle stage before writing: a cold lead, a trial user, and a paying customer
  never receive the same message.
- Subject 6 words or fewer, concrete; the first line is the point (it is also the preview
  text). Plain-text-looking email regularly beats designed email for founder-voice products.
- Send on behavior triggers (signed up, stalled, hit a limit) over calendar blasts.

## 6. CRO

- Changes come from evidence: session recordings, funnel drop-off data, user quotes, support
  questions. Redesigning without evidence is redecorating.
- One hypothesis per test, stated with its mechanism ("moving proof above the fold will lift
  starts because visitors bounce before seeing it").
- A page converting under about 1% on qualified traffic is an offer or positioning problem,
  not a page problem; no button test rescues a weak offer.
- Measure the money metric, not the click; respect sample-size honesty (data.md section 3);
  with small traffic, prefer big swings and qualitative signal over micro-tests.
- Forms: every field costs conversion; ask only what the next step truly needs.

## 7. Launches

- A launch is a week, not a day: warm the audience before, land the moment, follow with
  proof (usage, testimonials, answers to objections) after.
- The asset stack ready BEFORE launch day: positioning line, 60-90s demo, screenshots, FAQ,
  comparison page, and answers for "how is this different".
- Show up where the users already are (the specific communities, directories, newsletters for
  the niche); founder voice beats corporate voice everywhere it is allowed.
- Define launch success metrics beforehand (signups, trials, replies), or the retro is vibes.

## 8. Social and content distribution

- The hook line does 80% of the work: write 5 hooks per piece, pick against "would I stop
  scrolling".
- Native format per platform: rebuild the idea per platform rather than cross-posting one
  artifact everywhere.
- One pillar piece per week beats seven throwaways: make the deep asset, then derive clips,
  posts, and threads from it. Spend as long distributing a piece as making it (replies,
  communities, staggered reposts); publishing is not distribution.
- Consistency compounds; virality is a lottery ticket. Hold a cadence you can keep for 90 days.

## 9. Ban list (these also break trust, and some break law)

- Bought lists, follower buying, engagement pods, review astroturfing.
- Fake scarcity (countdown timers that reset), fake social proof, invented testimonials.
- Dark patterns: hidden unsubscribe, forced continuity, guilt-trip opt-outs.
- "We're excited to announce" openers; hashtag walls; keyword-stuffed pages.
- Trend openers ("in today's fast-paced world") and rhetorical-question hooks ("Tired of
  X?"): open with the one named reader's specific situation instead.
- The contrast tic ("It's not X. It's Y.") and triad slogans ("Faster. Smarter. Better."):
  replace with one concrete claim carrying a number.
- "Learn More" as a primary CTA: the CTA is a verb plus an outcome ("Start rendering").
- "Join thousands of..." proof you cannot source, and percent claims without a base ("300%
  more conversions"): use real counts and absolute numbers, or cut the claim.
- Clickbait the content does not cash: it trades one click for the reader's future trust.
- Metrics theater: reporting impressions where revenue was the goal.
- Ending a launch page on the platform-generic ask (star, subscribe, follow): the last line
  is the ownable sentence; the ask sits above it.

## 10. Verification checklist (every pass)

1. Stage and metric named for the asset? One CTA?
2. Message-match walk: ad (or post) to page to CTA reads as one promise, no pasted repeats?
3. Funnel leak table current, and the work aimed at the worst drop-off?
4. SEO: intent unique? SERP format matched? title/meta/H-structure checked? internal links
   in? schema honest?
5. Email: deliverability records verified (SPF/DKIM/DMARC), segments split by lifecycle,
   unsubscribe visible, seed-inbox test sent?
6. Tracking: UTMs consistent, money event fires (tested), dashboard shows the funnel?
7. Every claim passes writing.md's competitor test and ban list?
8. Every image src and internal link on the page resolves, img tags included (link checkers
   routinely skip them)?
9. Post-ship: actual numbers checked after launch (CTR, replies, conversions), not assumed?
